Executive Summary

The livability of a city refers to the people’s expression of valuing a place. In this project, our team explored how public art, more specifically the public art co-creation process, could measurably improve the livability of a city. Public art co-creation is unfamiliar to many, yet there are numerous possibilities within this realm that could help foster qualities that make cities more livable. Through contextual interviews, think-alouds, and storyboarding and speed dating, we found that people need motivation to enjoy the outcome and experience of co-creating. They also value working in a synchronized environment, which also gives them the sense of building the community. For people to participate in the art co-creation process, there needs to be a low threshold for collaboration. Additionally, negative critique is feared by the general public, so it is important that this is blocked. With these insights in mind, our team developed the idea of CreArt, an application that uses AR to allow friends and family to co-create art with friends and families on top of existing landmarks around the city. AR will allow for users to maintain immersion, controlling experiencer attention and thus allow for co-creation to take part naturally, without any collision.

My Role

Research lead and user researcher

As the research lead of the team, I was primarily in charge of handling and organizing the data the team collects. I made sure that each data collected was complete and error-free. I made sure that collected data from interviews were ready for analyzing and synthesizing.

I was also a user researcher on the team, conducting and notetaking multiple interviews and discovering insights.

Problem Space

How might we encourage the general public to take part in art co-creation project while feeling connected to the community and confident about expressing themselves?

Every form of art is very personal. Even community driven art is personal, but we still lack a system in which each individual creator can contribute in the creation process with lower threshold while enjoying themselves.

Methods

Contextual Inquiry

Our team interviewed five participants online to understand people’s attitude on public art in order to identify gaps or challenges and opportunities of the public art co-creation process. We asked semi-structured questions and probed interviewees to discover their feelings towards art. We then synthesized our results by conducting interpretation sessions for each interview and affinity diagramming.

This allowed us to learn more about the general public’s perspective on public art on accessibility and channels, hindrances for participating in the co-creation process, and their attitude on the overall co-creation process.

Think-Alouds

Our team also conducted think-alouds on an art co-creation game online based on a card game, A Fake Artist Goes to New York. The game divides players into artists and fake artists. Artists are given a word to draw, while fake artists guess the word. All players take turns and draw on a single canvas; we then gathered insights on users’ needs when collaborating. We synthesized our results using usability findings.

We found that...
  • Users require low threshold to participate in co-creation.
  • Users want to communicate with other visitors involved in the co-creation process, making the process and outcome more meaningful.
  • Users need to be intrinsically motivated or challenged in the co-creation process.

Storyboarding and Speed Dating

After series of interviews, our team moved forward to proposing potential solutions to determine the needs of users. We first found five potential areas of need and created three comic-based stories for each area, totaling to fifteen storyboards. In the speed dating sessions, interviewees were presented with these storyboards and we probed them for their feelings towards each storyboard.

The storyboards and speed dating sessions revealed to us how we would be able to construct a solution to the problem space, as well as showing areas that should be prioritized over others.

Insights

From the previously stated user research methodologies, our team discovered the following insights:

Motivation for Outcome and Process

People need motivation to enjoy the outcome and experience of co-creating. Friends and family members can also serve as motivation for people to participate in the art co-creation process. People also value the process more than the final product.

“I find the experience of creating most interesting...the outcome and the experience (process) of public art co-creation is also art.”

Building the Community through Interactions

People value working in a synchronized environment, which also gives them the sense of building the community. Synchronized collaboration enables interactions among participants and inspires creativity through real-time communication. People have more chances to interact with each other and thus generate more creative ideas through synchronized collaboration.

“I feel a sense of similarity and community as people who are just trying to collaborate and create art.”
“It’s not meaningful if each person creates independently since there is no understanding of how to work together.”

Low Threshold for Collaboration

There needs to be a low threshold for the art co-creation process for people to feel comfortable to participate in it. Each contributor should be able to express themselves alongside their friends and family, so they feel comfortable in this co-creation process.

“I feel motivated when I can express myself while contributing in the creation process.”

Negative Critique Unwelcome

People fear negative critique, so it’s important that people are not exposed this; otherwise, they would not feel comfortable in creating public art. Since art is very personal, people also feel that art critique is personal.

“I’m scared of critique from other people, so I tend to only share my projects to a few people.”

Solution

CreArt

In this project, we aimed to address the question: how might we encourage the general public to take part in art co-creation project while feeling connected to the community and confident about expressing themselves?

Our team proposed CreArt, a mobile app that allows users to co-create with their community and view other pieces of art in the city using augmented reality. We aimed to create an experience for users to build deeper connections with their community and with the city through public art that they and/ or other co-create.

This is a low fidelity prototype of CreArt.
